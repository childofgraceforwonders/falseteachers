Sermon Guide: Biblical Truth vs. Common Falsehoods

This guide is designed to help you navigate complex and often sensitive topics with biblical clarity and pastoral care. It breaks down common extra-biblical beliefs and doctrines, providing scriptural refutations to equip you for sound teaching. The goal is not condemnation, but compassionate correction, guiding believers back to the authority of Scripture.

Part I: Folk Beliefs & Popular Misconceptions

These are common sayings and ideas that sound "Christian" but lack biblical foundation. They often substitute cultural wisdom for divine truth.

"God helps those who help themselves."

The Claim: God's assistance is conditional upon human self-sufficiency and effort.

Common Practice: This phrase is often used to encourage self-reliance, implying that God is waiting for us to act first. It reflects a deistic or Pelagian worldview rather than one of grace.[1, 2]
Biblical Refutation: The Bible teaches the opposite. God helps the helpless. Salvation is an act of God's grace toward those who are spiritually dead and incapable of helping themselves.
"For while we were still weak, at the right time Christ died for the ungodly... God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us." ($Romans$ 5:6-8)
"For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast." ($Ephesians$ 2:8-9)
"God will never give you more than you can handle."

The Claim: God will not allow any suffering or trial to overwhelm a believer's personal strength.

Common Practice: Often offered as a word of comfort during suffering, this is a misinterpretation of 1 Corinthians 10:13, which specifically addresses *temptation*, not general hardship.[3, 1]
Biblical Refutation: The Bible shows that God often allows believers to be burdened *beyond their own strength* precisely so they learn to rely on His power, not their own.
"For we do not want you to be unaware, brothers, of the affliction we experienced in Asia. For we were so utterly burdened beyond our strength that we despaired of life itself... But that was to make us rely not on ourselves but on God who raises the dead." (2 $Corinthians$ 1:8-9)
"My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness." (2 $Corinthians$ 12:9)
"Cleanliness is next to Godliness."

The Claim: Physical cleanliness is a primary spiritual virtue.

Common Practice: This proverb, not found in the Bible, is used to promote physical hygiene as a spiritual discipline. While hygiene is good, this saying can wrongly elevate external appearance over internal purity.[1]
Biblical Refutation: Jesus repeatedly prioritized internal, moral purity over external, ritual cleanliness. He rebuked the Pharisees for focusing on the outside while their hearts were corrupt.
"Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you clean the outside of the cup and the plate, but inside they are full of greed and self-indulgence. You blind Pharisee! First clean the inside of the cup and the plate, that the outside also may be clean." ($Matthew$ 23:25-26)
The "Sinner's Prayer" as a Guarantee of Salvation

The Claim: Reciting a specific prayer formula guarantees salvation.

Common Practice: The use of an "altar call" followed by a formulaic "sinner's prayer" is a modern revivalist invention.[4] It can create false assurance based on an event rather than genuine conversion.[5]
Biblical Refutation: Salvation involves genuine, heartfelt belief and public confession, evidenced by a transformed life ("fruit in keeping with repentance"). The Bible never prescribes a magical prayer for salvation.
"If you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved." ($Romans$ 10:9-10)
"Bear fruit in keeping with repentance." ($Matthew$ 3:8)

Part II: The Prosperity Gospel

This teaching asserts that it is always God's will for believers to be wealthy and healthy, and that faith is a force to unlock these blessings. It is a distortion of the biblical gospel.[6, 7]

Core Tenet: The "Seed-Faith" Doctrine

The Claim: Giving money to a ministry ("sowing a seed") obligates God to provide a multiplied financial return.[2]

Common Practice: Televangelists and prosperity preachers solicit donations with promises of a "hundred-fold return," turning giving into a transactional investment scheme rather than an act of worship.[8, 9, 10] This is especially prevalent in ministries like Winners' Chapel and Christ Embassy.[11, 12, 13]
Biblical Refutation: This doctrine twists scripture by taking verses out of context.
  • Luke 6:38 ("Give, and it will be given to you"): The context is about giving mercy and forgiveness, not money for financial gain.[2, 8]
  • Mark 4 (Parable of the Sower): Jesus explicitly states the "seed" is the Word of God, and the "harvest" is salvation and a changed life, not a financial windfall.[2, 14, 8]
  • 2 Corinthians 9:6-12: Paul discusses financial giving, but the "harvest" is one of "righteousness" and "thanksgiving to God," not personal enrichment for the donor.[2, 8, 15] The apostles themselves were often poor (1 Cor. 4:11).[2]
Core Tenet: The Atonement Covers Financial Poverty

The Claim: Jesus' death on the cross was not only for sin but also to deliver believers from the "curse" of poverty.[16]

Common Practice: Teachers like Kenneth Copeland claim Jesus's death frees us from poverty. They misinterpret 2 Corinthians 8:9 ("though he was rich, yet for your sakes he became poor") to support this.[16, 17]
Biblical Refutation: The context of 2 Corinthians 8 is an appeal for sacrificial giving. Christ's "richness" was His divine glory; His "poverty" was the incarnation. The "richness" we receive is spiritual salvation, not material wealth.[16, 17] Jesus came to "save his people from their sins" ($Matthew$ 1:21), not their debts.
Early Church Rebuttal on Wealth & Suffering

The prosperity gospel is diametrically opposed to the teachings of the early church fathers.

On Wealth: They saw wealth not as a right, but as a dangerous responsibility belonging to the poor. Hoarding wealth was seen as theft. "The bread in your larder belongs to the hungry. The cloak in your wardrobe belongs to the naked... You do injustice to every man whom you could help but do not." - Basil the Great (c. 330-379).[18, 8, 19] "You are not making a gift of your possession to the poor person. You are handing over to him what is his." - Ambrose of Milan (c. 340-397).[19]
On Suffering: They saw suffering and persecution not as a curse or lack of faith, but as the normative Christian experience and a privilege to imitate Christ. "Christianity is greatest when it is hated by the world." - Ignatius of Antioch (c. 35-108).[20] The idea that suffering is a sign of weak faith is a cruel and anti-biblical invention.[20, 21, 22, 23]

Part III: Generational Curses

This doctrine claims Christians can be held in bondage by the sins of their ancestors, requiring special rituals for freedom. This teaching is especially prevalent in Africa, as it syncretizes with traditional worldviews.[24]

The Doctrine of Inherited Bondage

The Claim: A Christian's life can be negatively controlled by curses or demonic oppression resulting from sins committed by ancestors.[25, 26]

Common Practice: Proponents teach that Christ's death is insufficient for these "curses." Adherents must perform elaborate, extra-biblical rituals: researching ancestral sins, confessing them on behalf of the dead, and renouncing the curses to break free.[25, 27] This creates a "Jesus-plus" gospel of fear.[25]
Biblical Refutation: This doctrine is built on misreading Old Testament texts while ignoring the New Covenant's finality.
  • Misreading Exodus 20:5: The verse says God visits iniquity on the "third and fourth generation of those who *hate me*." This is not a curse on righteous children, but a warning of consequences for generations that *continue* in their ancestors' rebellion.[25, 26, 24, 28]
  • Principle of Personal Responsibility: The Bible could not be clearer that each person is accountable for their own sin. God explicitly refutes the idea of inherited guilt.
    "Fathers shall not be put to death for their sons, nor shall sons be put to death for their fathers. Each one shall be put to death for his own sin." ($Deuteronomy$ 24:16).[24, 29]
    "The soul who sins shall die. The son shall not suffer for the iniquity of the father..." ($Ezekiel$ 18:4, 20).[19, 30, 31]
  • The Sufficiency of Christ: For the believer, this entire debate is settled at the cross.
    "Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us." ($Galatians$ 3:13).[26, 30]
    "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come." (2 $Corinthians$ 5:17).[20, 29]
    Our identity is in Christ, not our ancestry. While the *consequences* of a sinful family background (e.g., learned behaviors, addiction) are real, they are dealt with through sanctification and discipleship, not curse-breaking rituals.[20, 30, 29]

Part IV: A Look at Specific Nigerian Movements

Nigeria's vibrant Christian landscape includes movements with unique and sometimes problematic doctrines that blend biblical language with cultural or extra-biblical ideas.

Celestial Church of Christ (CCC)

The Claim: The church's doctrines, rituals, and rules were given directly by divine revelation to its founder, S.B.J. Oshoffa, and are therefore infallible.[32, 33, 34]

Common Practice: The CCC enforces a strict set of extra-biblical rules derived from this alleged revelation, which often mix Christian terms with Old Testament ceremonial law and other concepts.[35, 3, 36, 31, 37, 38, 39, 40, 21, 41, 42] Examples include:
  • Women are considered unclean and barred from church during menstruation and for 40 days after childbirth.[36, 41]
  • Members are forbidden from eating pork.[36, 41]
  • Wearing shoes inside the church is forbidden.[36, 41]
  • Specific colors of clothing (black and red) are prohibited.[36, 41]
Biblical Refutation: These practices place believers back under the Old Testament ceremonial law, which was fulfilled in Christ. The New Covenant brings freedom from such regulations.
"Therefore let no one pass judgment on you in questions of food and drink, or with regard to a festival or a new moon or a Sabbath. These are a shadow of the things to come, but the substance belongs to Christ." ($Colossians$ 2:16-17)
The claim of a new, binding revelation outside of Scripture undermines the principle of *Sola Scriptura* and the finality of the biblical canon.
Christ Embassy (LoveWorld Inc.)

The Claim: Pastor Chris Oyakhilome presents several doctrines that deviate significantly from orthodox Christianity, including teachings on prosperity, the nature of Christ, and sinlessness.

Common Practice & Refutation:
  • Extreme Prosperity: Teaches that believers are entitled to immense wealth, with Pastor Chris claiming his name is on "every dollar".[43, 25] Refutation: This is a form of the prosperity gospel, which the Bible warns against (1 Timothy 6:5-10).
  • "Born Again" Jesus: Teaches the heresy that Jesus, being sinless, had to be "born again" after His resurrection.[43, 25] Refutation: Regeneration ("born again") is for sinful humanity ($John$ 3:3). Jesus was the perfect, sinless lamb of God ($Hebrews$ 4:15; 1 $Peter$ 1:19) and had no need of it. This doctrine undermines His perfect sacrifice.
  • Sinless Perfection: Teaches that the "new creation" is an entirely new species that is no longer a sinner and is as righteous as Jesus.[44, 45, 15] Refutation: While believers are *declared* righteous (justification), we are still in a lifelong *process* of being made holy (sanctification) and struggle with the flesh ($Romans$ 7:15-25; $Philippians$ 3:12).

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is it wrong for a Christian to be wealthy or to desire prosperity?

A: No, wealth itself is not sinful. The Bible includes examples of godly people who were wealthy, like Abraham. The error of the Prosperity Gospel is not in acknowledging that God can bless people financially, but in its core tenets: 1) It teaches that wealth is a *right* for all believers, making poverty a sign of weak faith.[46, 37] 2) It turns giving into a transactional tool to manipulate God for financial gain, rather than an act of worship.[2, 8] 3) It redefines the gospel's purpose from salvation from sin to salvation from poverty.[1, 17] The biblical and early church view is that wealth is a dangerous tool to be stewarded for the glory of God and the care of the poor, not a sign of spiritual superiority.[16, 47, 48, 18, 8, 19, 41, 49, 50, 51]

Q: My family has a long history of addiction and abuse. Isn't that a generational curse?

A: The Bible makes a clear distinction between a theological "curse" and the real-world *consequences* of sin that pass through generations. A family background of addiction or abuse creates deep wounds, learned behaviors, and a greater propensity toward those same sins.[24, 29] These are serious spiritual and psychological realities that must be addressed through repentance, discipleship, counseling, and the sanctifying work of the Holy Spirit. However, this is different from a "curse" that gives demons a legal right over you. For a believer in Christ, all curses have been broken at the cross ($Galatians$ 3:13).[26, 30] You are a new creation (2 $Corinthians$ 5:17), and your identity is in Christ, not your family history. You are responsible for your own choices, and through Christ, you have the power to break the cycle of sinful behavior.[30, 29, 31]

Q: Are all traditions not found in the Bible bad?

A: No. It's crucial to distinguish between what is *extra-biblical* (outside the Bible) and what is *anti-biblical* (against the Bible). Many traditions are simply cultural or practical applications that don't contradict Scripture. For example, wearing a wedding ring is an extra-biblical tradition that reflects the biblical value of marriage.[52] Celebrating Christmas on December 25th is a tradition that honors the biblical event of the Incarnation.[52] These traditions become a problem only when they are elevated to the level of divine law or used to judge others' spirituality. The false teachings discussed in this guide are largely *anti-biblical* because they directly contradict or distort core doctrines of the faith.[1, 39]
